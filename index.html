Week 9

Reading Response

I would accept the offer from Pepsi and use Karl Marx’s idea of commodity fetishism. I would glamourize Pepsi drinks by posting pictures where I look stylish and inspirational, and just happen to be holding a Pepsi drink in my hand. That way I am selling my brand and lifestyle and showing that it includes the Pepsi drink. That way it creates a false psychological need for my followers, who are inspired by my aesthetic and lifestyle to want to buy Pepsi products. We live in a capitalist society and all of the media we consume is trying to sell us something, so why can’t I profit from it?

Week 8

Reading Response

This situation makes me think about Donna Haraway’s words in her “Cyborg Manifesto” where she wrote that “The new economic and technological arrangements are also related to the collapsing welfare state and the ensuing intensification of demands on women to sustain daily life for themselves as well as for men, children, and old people. The feminization of poverty-- generated by dismantling the welfare state, by the homework economy where stable jobs become the exception, and sustained by the expectation that women's wages will not be matched by a male income for the support of children-- has become an urgent focus. The causes of various women-headed households are a function of race, class, or sexuality; but their increasing generality is a ground for coalitions of women on many issues. That women regularly sustain daily life partly as a function of their enforced status as mothers is hardly new; the kind of integration with the overall capitalist and progressively war-based economy is new. The particular pressure, for example, on US black women, who have achieved an escape from (barely) paid domestic service and who now hold clerical and similar jobs in large numbers, has large implications for continued enforced black poverty with employment. Teenage women in industrializing areas of the Third World increasingly find themselves the sole or major source of a cash wage for their families. At the same time, access to land is ever more problematic. These developments must have major consequences in the psychodynamics and politics of gender and race.” I would bring this to the attention of the HR department and I would even make the point of showing them her paper. A successful tech company should not be contributing to the gender wage gap. I would ask for the company to take more initiative to hire more women-identifying employees. Many tech companies have internships and programs aimed at hiring and training minorities, and I would propose that my company do the same.

Week 7

Reading Response

I would start by asking myself the same questions Allison Parrish raises in her lecture “Programming is Forgetting: Toward a New Hacker Ethic.”: Who gets to use what I make? Who am I leaving out? How does what I make facilitate or hinder access? What data am I using? Whose labor produced it and what biases and assumptions are built into it? Why choose this particular phenomenon for digitization or transcription? And what do the data leave out? What systems of authority am I enacting through what I make? What systems of support do I rely on? How does what I make support other people? Based on my answers to these questions I believe that the ethical thing to do is to leak the vaccine research so that it is available to people all around the world. In fear that if it is leaked publicly too quickly, it will be taken down quickly from the internet, I would take some precautionary steps. Before leaking it publicly, I would reach out to scientists, maybe even at other universities, in undeveloped countries and share the vaccine research with them privately. That way many people already have access to it outside of Omnicorp. Then, I would leak it to the public.

Week 6

Reading Response

I would create an internship program for Conkers University students at Omnicorp. Through this internship the students would have access to a state of the art computation lab that has much more expensive resources than at Conker’s University. In exchange, all the work the students create during the internship is owned by the company of Omnicorp. This way, their works go from being “public goods” to “private goods”. As an incentive, if their work is successful they will be offered a full time position at the company.

Week 5

Reading Response

I would create a lesson focused on collaboration, and how it has developed throughout the history of the internet. I would show the students the first personal computers in the Doug Engelbart video to show them how teleconferencing and sharing screens began. Then I would show them Zoom and Microsoft Teams from the early 2020’s. I would also introduce them to Ted Nelson’s Xanadu project. In the Ted Nelson reading, he discusses the concept of “computer-assisted instruction” and how he envisioned computers being used to teach. I would show my students the progression of online university programs and how the creation and development of the Internet have made learning more accessible to all.

Reading Response

Art plays a central role in all cultures. I think that it was inevitable that once the internet became widely accessible, artists would begin to experiment and build communities online. I believe that an artist's success can be defined by if their work impacts at least one other individual. I believe that all of the websites from the early web and Geo cities should be considered successful because they clearly impacted people in a way that made them want to save them. My favorite piece of internet art from the reading was Olia Lialina’s “My Boyfriend Came Back From the War”. Some of my favorite pieces of internet art are the Barbie Flash Player games I used to play online as a child. Although they are online games, I still count them as art as they had a huge impact on my childhood.

Week 3

Reading Response

I was very inspired by Detroit’s Equitable Internet Initiative and their approach to making the Internet accessible to all by teaching community members how to build their own wireless networks. I would not only do this but also start an initiative to teach people how to program so that our country could have its own major online services created by the people in our country. This would create lots of jobs and build a local tech economy. So not only would people have their own high-speed internet, but we would also have our own forms of social media, email, streaming, gaming, etc.

Week 2

Reading Response

Roy Rosenzweig, Writing the History of the Internet (1998)

image from The Museum of Modern Art

I am curious to know what the internet would look like if it were not funded and created for military purposes. Would the internet even exist without the government's efforts to win the cold war? What stood out to me the most during this reading was how all of the names mentioned were men. When reading about the creation of the internet, I found it funny that emails were originally built as a way for young men to discuss “science fiction, football, ham radios, cars, chess, and bridge”. These young men had elite access to the ARPANET because they were at elite academic institutions and were given opportunities that women did not have access to. If I could go back in time, I would make sure that women were equally as involved in engineering and computer science. I would probably focus on the universities and change the past so that women would be allowed to attend the universities so if they wished to work in computer science they could. When I researched on my own I found out that In 1969, Yale became the first Ivy League university to allow female students. After that, a lot of other Ivy League colleges began to welcome women, but slowly. For example, women were not allowed at Columbia University until 1983. (https://historycollection.com/40-basic-rights-women-did-not-have-until-the-1970s/15/) In response to the “great man theory” of history, I noticed that although the creation of the internet has several big names leading the projects, it seemed to me like it was a very collaborative effort. Students and scientists from all over the country used the ARPANET to experiment and develop new ways of communication. It was not just created by one genius. And these men involved were given the opportunity due to their circumstances, not just because they were "great men".
